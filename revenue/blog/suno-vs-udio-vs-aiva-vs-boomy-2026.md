# Suno vs Udio vs Aiva vs Boomy (2026): Which AI Music Generator Actually Ships a Track?

If you're building a [faceless content channel](./faceless-ai-content-channel.md) or running [zero-cost digital products that sell](./zero-cost-digital-products-that-sell.md), audio is usually the asset you quietly outsource or steal — and both options hurt. The question every solo operator hits now is the same: *which AI music generator do I actually build on so the soundtrack stops being a blocker?*

So this is an operator's comparison, not a leaderboard. We put the four tools that actually matter in 2026 — **Suno (one-shot full songs with vocals), Udio (high-fidelity genre tracks), Aiva (cinematic scoring you can edit), and Boomy (fastest generate-to-streaming loop)** — through the only lens that counts for a small business or solo founder: *can you ship a track that fits the video, at a cost you'll still pay in month three, and does it move the funnel or just pad a "content creation" bill?*

## The 30-second version

- **Suno:** the one-shot song machine — type a vibe and get a complete track with vocals and structure; best when you need a finished song (hook, verse, drop) fast and don't care about stems.
- **Udio:** the fidelity-first generator — genre-accurate, musical output with strong extend/remix controls; best when the track has to *sound* like the genre, not just be labeled as it.
- **Aiva:** the composer's editor — orchestral/cinematic scoring on a timeline you can actually edit; best when the job is instrumental score, not a pop song, and you want royalty-clear control.
- **Boomy:** the publish loop — generate, tweak, and push to streaming in minutes; best when you want volume background music *and* a shot at streaming royalties without touching a DAW.

None is "the best." They solve different jobs. The rest tells you exactly which to reach for.

Read that table as "what is each one's superpower," not "who scores highest." Your job is to match the superpower to the track you actually need this week — a hook for a Reel, a bed for a tutorial, or a score for a documentary cut.

## Match the tool to the job

### 1. A finished song in one prompt → **Suno**
Faceless channels and ad creatives need a hook that *sounds produced*, not a 12-second loop, and Suno's full-song output (intro, verse, chorus, drop) drops straight under a video. That's the same discipline the [content repurposing engine](./content-repurposing-engine-2026.md) needs before it spins one asset into ten formats — generate the hero track once, then distribute. Use it for the pieces that have to feel like a record, not for subtle background. This is the audio half of [packaging and selling AI prompts](./package-and-sell-ai-prompts.md): make the track once, ship it as a product forever.

### 2. The genre has to sound right → **Udio**
When the brief is "lo-fi hip-hop that actually grooves" or "synthwave that doesn't sound like a ringtone," Udio's musicality and extend/remix controls beat a generic preset bed. It pairs with the positioning logic from [how to run an AI company on zero budget](./how-to-run-ai-company-zero-budget.md): the track is the proof the content sounds intentional, not auto-generated. It's the same quality bar as the [AI lead-generation system](./build-ai-lead-generation-system-2026.md) — the asset has to read (and here, sound) like a human made it on purpose.

### 3. Cinematic score you can edit → **Aiva**
If the job is a YouTube intro, a product reveal, or a documentary bed where you need to hit a cut on beat two, Aiva's timeline editing and orchestral renders give you control the song generators never will. That maps onto the [faceless content channel strategy](./faceless-youtube-vs-tiktok-vs-newsletter-2026.md) written half: the score that makes the newsletter teaser feel premium. It won't sing you a pop hook, but it will clear the licensing anxiety the others leave hanging.

### 4. Volume background + a shot at royalties → **Boomy**
When the job is "fill 40 videos with non-copyright-strikable music and maybe earn a streaming dime," Boomy's generate-to-publish loop removes the DAW tax entirely. That's the planning complement to the [autonomous AI business stack](./autonomous-ai-business-stack-2026.md): know the output format (streaming-ready) before you spend the credits. It's the cheapest of the four, so it only pays when the volume is already high.

## A realistic cost picture (2026)

- **Suno:** free tier to try, paid for higher credits and commercial volume — you're paying for finished songs with vocals; it replaces a stock-music subscription the first time a track clears a claim.
- **Udio:** free tier, paid for more generations and stem access — you're paying for musicality and control; worth it when genre fit is the difference between a scroll-past and a save.
- **Aiva:** paid with a free trial, licensing tiers for commercial use — you're paying for editable, royalty-clear score; it only earns its price when the brief is instrumental and broadcast-grade.
- **Boomy:** cheapest entry, with a revenue share on streaming — you're paying for the publish loop; the bill stays low because the job is contained and the upside is royalties, not just usage.

Rule of thumb: if the track is *the hook that sells the video*, Suno or Udio earn their price on the first clip that converts. The moment the job is *score or volume*, Aiva or Boomy pay for themselves by giving you editability or distribution. Same economic logic we lay out for [self-hosted stacks](./autonomous-ai-business-stack-2026.md): pay for the tool whose bill scales with *shipped content that earns*, not with *experimentation*.

## The decision shortcut

> **Pick Suno** if you want a complete song with vocals from one prompt.
> **Pick Udio** if the genre has to sound authentic and you want extend/remix control.
> **Pick Aiva** if you need editable, royalty-clear cinematic scoring.
> **Pick Boomy** if you want the fastest generate-to-streaming loop and volume background music.

Most solo creators we know run a **Suno-for-hooks + Boomy-for-background** split, and reach for **Udio or Aiva** only when the genre has to be convincing or the brief is cinematic. You're not loyal to a music app — you're loyal to the track that fits the cut and the margin.
