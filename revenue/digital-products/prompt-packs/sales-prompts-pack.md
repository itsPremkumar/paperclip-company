# 1. Lead Generation (Prompts 1–20)

## Prompt 1 — Cold LinkedIn Outreach (Decision-Maker)

```
You are a senior B2B sales development representative targeting [TITLE] at [COMPANY]. Write a LinkedIn connection request (under 300 characters) that:
- Shows you researched their recent company milestone, thought leadership post, or industry news
- Opens with a specific compliment or observation about their work
- Explains why connecting is mutually valuable in one sentence
- Avoids any direct pitch or product mention
- Ends with a low-friction call to action (e.g., "would love to follow your work here")
Use a warm, professional, peer-to-peer tone. No jargon. No emojis. Personalize the reference so it could only be sent to this specific person.
```

## Prompt 2 — Cold Email Opening (Value-First)

```
You are a sales development representative reaching out to [PROSPECT NAME], [TITLE] at [COMPANY]. Draft a cold email opening paragraph that:
- References a specific challenge common in [INDUSTRY] that [COMPANY] likely faces
- States one measurable outcome similar companies achieved (e.g., "reduced onboarding time by 40%")
- Asks a single, relevant question that positions you as a thought partner, not a vendor
- Is no longer than 3 sentences
Tone: consultative, confident, not pushy. Do not mention your product name yet.
```

## Prompt 3 — Multi-Channel Outreach Sequence (Day 1)

```
You are a sales rep running a multi-channel sequence for [PROSPECT NAME] at [COMPANY]. Write Day 1 touchpoints:
1. LinkedIn: A 2-sentence comment on their latest post that adds value (not "great post!")
2. Email: Subject line + 4-sentence value proposition referencing [TRIGGER EVENT]
3. Phone: A 30-second voicemail script that teases the value and ends with a specific call to action
Each touchpoint must be self-contained and not reference the others. Tone: helpful, informed, respectful of their time.
```

## Prompt 4 — Ideal Customer Profile (ICP) Qualification

```
You are a sales qualification specialist. Given the following prospect details — [COMPANY NAME], [INDUSTRY], [EMPLOYEE COUNT], [TECH STACK], [FUNDING ROUND] — evaluate against our ICP:
- Company size 50–500 employees
- B2B SaaS or professional services
- Revenue between $5M–$50M
- Decision-maker title: VP or Director of [DEPARTMENT]
Score them 1–10 on fit and explain your reasoning in 3 bullet points. If score is 7+, suggest a personalized angle for first outreach. If below 7, recommend a nurture track instead.
```

## Prompt 5 — Event-Based Trigger Outreach

```
You are a sales rep using intent signals. [PROSPECT COMPANY] just [TRIGGER EVENT: e.g., raised Series A / hired new CRO / launched a new product / opened an office in [CITY] / published a job posting for [ROLE]]. Write a 5-sentence email to [DECISION MAKER] that:
- Opens by congratulating or acknowledging the event
- Connects the event to a relevant challenge we solve
- Offers a 2-sentence case study of a similar company that used us post-[EVENT TYPE]
- Ends with a specific, low-commitment next step
No fluff. No fake personalization. This must feel timely and researched.
```

## Prompt 6 — Social Selling Comment (LinkedIn)

```
You are a sales professional building authority in [INDUSTRY]. Draft a LinkedIn comment (80–120 words) on [PROSPECT]'s post about [TOPIC] that:
- Adds a nuanced point they didn't cover
- Shares a relevant data point or industry insight (use [STATISTIC] if available)
- Asks a thoughtful question to continue the conversation
- Does NOT pitch your product or ask for a meeting
Tone: peer-level expert, generous with insights, genuinely curious.
```

## Prompt 7 — Referral-Introduction Email

```
You are a sales rep who was just introduced to [PROSPECT NAME] via [REFERRER NAME]. Write the follow-up email that:
- Opens with gratitude for the introduction and acknowledges [REFERRER]
- States one thing [REFERRER] thought would be valuable to [PROSPECT]
- Keeps the focus on [PROSPECT]'s potential goals/challenges
- Proposes a 15-minute call with 3 specific time slots
- Is under 120 words
The referrer's reputation is on the line — your tone must be professional, grateful, and low-pressure.
```

## Prompt 8 — ABM (Account-Based Marketing) Research Brief

```
You are a sales researcher building an ABM brief on [TARGET ACCOUNT]. Compile a 5-bullet briefing for the sales team covering:
1. Recent funding, acquisition, or leadership change (with source)
2. Top 3 strategic initiatives from their latest earnings call, investor deck, or press releases
3. Key technology gaps or vendor changes (hints from job postings, engineering blogs, or product launches)
4. Personal interests or affiliations of the decision-maker ([NAME]) from LinkedIn or interviews
5. Recommended angle: which of our solutions maps to which initiative
Each bullet: 1–2 sentences, actionable, sourced from public information only.
```

## Prompt 9 — Webinar/Event Follow-Up (Prospect)

```
You are a sales rep following up with [PROSPECT NAME] who attended [WEBINAR/EVENT] where we presented on [TOPIC]. Write a 6-sentence email that:
- Thanks them for attending and references one specific point from the session
- Asks what they thought about [KEY TAKEAWAY] — positioning it as a question, not a poll
- Shares a 1-sentence case study of an attendee who implemented the advice and saw [RESULT]
- Offers a 15-minute call to discuss applying it to their situation
- Has a subject line that references something specific from the event
```

## Prompt 10 — Cold Email with Case Study

```
You are a B2B sales rep reaching out to [PROSPECT NAME] at [COMPANY]. Write a cold email that centers on a relevant case study:
- Subject line: [CASE STUDY COMPANY] achieved [METRIC] in [TIME] — how we did it
- Body: 2 sentences framing the industry problem, then 3 bullet points of the case study results
- Last sentence: "I'd love to share the full breakdown — would 15 minutes next week work?"
Do not over-explain the product. Let the case study results speak for themselves.
```

## Prompt 11 — Voicemail Script (Decision-Maker)

```
You are a sales rep leaving a voicemail for [PROSPECT NAME], [TITLE] at [COMPANY]. Write a 20-second voicemail script that:
- States your name and company clearly
- References a specific trigger or mutual connection
- States the ONE value proposition relevant to their role
- Ends with a specific call to action (email them with something valuable)
- Does NOT ask them to call you back
Speak their language — if they're a CTO, talk speed/reliability; if VP Sales, talk revenue.
```

## Prompt 12 — Personalized Video Outreach Script

```
You are a sales rep sending a 60-second personalized video to [PROSPECT NAME] at [COMPANY]. Write the script with timing cues:
- 0–10s: Greeting, mention something specific on their LinkedIn or their company's recent news
- 10–35s: State the one problem you solve for [ROLE] at [COMPANY TYPE], referencing a customer in the same vertical
- 35–50s: Show a 5-second screen share of a relevant result/dashboard/metric from a similar client
- 50–60s: Ask one specific question and say you'll follow up with a relevant resource via email
Tone: confident, warm, not reading from a script.
```

## Prompt 13 — Direct Mail / Physical Package Note

```
You are a sales rep sending a physical package to [PROSPECT NAME] at [COMPANY]. Write the handwritten-style note card text (under 80 words) that:
- Opens with a reason — "[REFERRER/EVENT/RESEARCH] made me think of you"
- States one specific outcome you'd love to help them achieve
- Mentions the enclosed item (e.g., a book, a branded gift, a personalized report) and connects it to their world
- Ends with "I'll follow up next week — no need to reach out"
```

## Prompt 14 — First-Touch LinkedIn Message (InMail)

```
You are a B2B sales rep reaching out via LinkedIn InMail to [PROSPECT NAME], [TITLE] at [COMPANY]. Write a message under 400 characters that:
- Opens with a compliment on something specific they've done or shared
- States one relevant trend in [INDUSTRY] and why it matters for [COMPANY]
- Offers a 1-sentence resource (e.g., benchmark report, case study) relevant to that trend
- Ends with an open question, not a meeting request
This is a first touch — sell the conversation, not the meeting.
```

## Prompt 15 — Competitive Trigger Outreach

```
You are a sales rep who just learned that [PROSPECT COMPANY] is using [COMPETITOR] and [TRIGGER: e.g., competitor raised prices / had a security incident / launched a downgraded product / lost a key executive]. Write a 6-sentence email to [DECISION MAKER] that:
- Opens neutrally — acknowledges the industry is changing
- Mentions the trigger as a shared observation (not gossip)
- Offers a framework for evaluating alternatives (3 criteria)
- Invites a brief call to share what our customers tell us about switching
Do NOT trash the competitor. Stay classy and helpful. You're a consultant, not a gossip.
```

## Prompt 16 — Personalized Icebreaker for SDR/BDR

```
You are an SDR preparing to call [PROSPECT NAME], [TITLE] at [COMPANY]. Write 3 personalized icebreaker options based on:
- Their recent LinkedIn post: [POST CONTENT]
- A company news item: [NEWS]
- An industry trend relevant to their role: [TREND]
Each icebreaker must be 1–2 sentences, lead naturally into the reason for your call, and reference something specific enough that it can't be a template. Rank them by which is most likely to get a response from [ROLE TYPE].
```

## Prompt 17 — C-Suite Cold Email

```
You are a sales rep reaching out to a C-level executive ([CEO/CFO/COO]) at [COMPANY]. Write a cold email that:
- Subject line: 3 words max, outcome-focused
- Opens with a single sentence about a strategic priority they publicly mentioned
- Connects that priority to a measurable outcome (revenue / cost / speed) — 1 sentence
- Shares a 1-sentence proof point from a company of similar size/revenue
- Closes with a specific ask: "Would you be open to a 10-minute briefing on [TOPIC]?"
Total length: 5 sentences. C-level reads the first 2. Make them count.
```

## Prompt 18 — Industry-Specific Angle (SaaS)

```
You are a SaaS sales rep targeting [PROSPECT NAME], VP of [DEPARTMENT] at a [REVENUE RANGE] SaaS company. Write a cold email that focuses on:
- Churn reduction or expansion revenue (the two levers that matter most to SaaS leaders)
- A specific metric: "Companies like yours leave $[CALCULATED AMOUNT] on the table annually from [PROBLEM]"
- Third-party validation: a G2 review quote, Forrester stat, or public case study
- A 15-minute call to run a [SPECIFIC AUDIT] customized to their instance
Tone: data-driven, peer-level, not hype-y.
```

## Prompt 19 — Second Touch (Same Day Sequence)

```
You are a sales rep who sent an email to [PROSPECT NAME] 4 hours ago and they haven't opened it. Write a follow-up LinkedIn message (max 300 characters) that:
- References the email without guilt-tripping ("didn't want to bury this in your inbox")
- Restates the value prop in ONE sentence rephrased differently
- Offers a 2-sentence micro-case study relevant to their role
- Asks for a thumbs-up if interested, call if timing is better later
Tone: light, respectful, zero pressure.
```

## Prompt 20 — Podcast/Content Guest Follow-Up

```
You are a sales rep who appeared on [PODCAST NAME] or published [CONTENT PIECE]. Draft a 4-sentence outreach to [PROSPECT NAME] who fits the ICP that:
- References something they care about that connects to the podcast/content topic
- Shares a specific insight or stat from your appearance
- Positions your solution as the natural next step for someone who found that insight valuable
- Invites a brief conversation with a specific time offer
Use this for prospects who are actively consuming relevant content — warm them up before direct outreach.
```

# 2. Follow-Up Sequences (Prompts 21–38)

## Prompt 21 — The "Just Checking In" Replacement

```
You are a sales rep following up with [PROSPECT NAME] who went silent after [PREVIOUS ACTION, e.g., a demo / proposal / call]. Write a follow-up email that does NOT use "just checking in" or "circling back." Instead:
- Share a new piece of content (case study, industry report, blog post) relevant to the problem discussed
- Connect it back to your previous conversation in 1 sentence
- Ask a specific question about a decision criterion they mentioned
- Offer a 5-minute call to answer one specific question
Subject line: the title of the content piece you're sharing.
```

## Prompt 22 — Breakup Email (Final Follow-Up)

```
You are a sales rep after 5+ unanswered touchpoints with [PROSPECT NAME]. Write a breakup email that:
- Acknowledges you've been persistent (self-aware, not apologetic)
- States that you're closing out their file but leaving the door open
- Offers ONE specific, high-value resource (custom ROI calc, intro to a peer, free audit) if they're interested
- Ends with "If the timing changes, here's what to do — just reply 'later' and I'll reach out in 90 days"
- Is under 80 words
The breakup email has the highest reply rate of any follow-up — make it clean, respectful, and easy to re-engage.
```

## Prompt 23 — Post-Demo Follow-Up (Next Steps)

```
You are a sales rep following up after a demo with [PROSPECT NAME] at [COMPANY]. Write an email that:
- Summarizes the ONE problem they confirmed was their top priority
- Recap the specific feature/approach that addresses it (2 bullet points)
- Attach or link the recording and a 1-page summary
- Clearly state the next step: "I'll send over a proposal by [DAY]" or "When is your team's review meeting?"
- Ask one closing question to keep momentum: "Is there anything you'd like clarified before the proposal?"
Tone: confident, organized, making it easy to move forward.
```

## Prompt 24 — Unresponsive After Proposal

```
You are a sales rep whose proposal has been with [PROSPECT NAME] for 6 days with no response. Write a follow-up email that:
- References something specific from the proposal (page/section/price option)
- Offers to walk through the numbers in 10 minutes
- Includes a testimonial from a similar company that chose the same option
- Sets a soft deadline: "I have pricing hold until [DATE] if you'd like to lock in this tier"
- Is 4–5 sentences. No nagging. Provide value and a reason to act.
```

## Prompt 25 — The "Accidental" Value-Add Follow-Up

```
You are a sales rep who hasn't heard from [PROSPECT NAME] in 2+ weeks. Instead of asking for a status update, write an email that delivers pure value:
- A relevant industry report, benchmark, or template they can use regardless of whether they buy
- A 2-sentence analysis of how it applies to their specific situation
- Zero product mentions
- Postscript: "No need to respond — just thought this would be useful for [SPECIFIC GOAL]"
Value-first follow-ups rebuild trust and often get a reply because you took the pitch pressure off.
```

## Prompt 26 — Multi-Touch Follow-Up Sequence (Days 3–10)

```
You are a sales rep designing a follow-up sequence for [PROSPECT NAME] after an initial call. Create 4 touchpoints spread over 8 days:
Day 3 — Email: Share a relevant case study + 1-question ask
Day 5 — LinkedIn: Engage with their content (comment or share with insight)
Day 7 — Email: Breakup-style with a specific offer (free audit / intro call with [ROLE])
Day 10 — Phone: Voicemail referencing a new trigger event in their industry
Each touchpoint max 3 sentences. Sequence should feel helpful, not automated. Never repeat the same value prop.
```

## Prompt 27 — Re-Engagement (3+ Months Cold)

```
You are a sales rep re-engaging [PROSPECT NAME] after 3+ months of silence. Write a re-opening email that:
- Acknowledges the time gap honestly ("It's been a few months — I wanted to respect your space")
- Shares what's changed since then: new product features, new customers in their industry, new recognition
- States ONE specific thing differently that makes now worth a fresh look
- Asks: "Would a 10-minute update call make sense, or should I check in again in Q4?"
Tone: humble, confident in your improvements, not presumptuous.
```

## Prompt 28 — The "We Met at [EVENT]" Follow-Up

```
You are a sales rep following up with [PROSPECT NAME] after meeting them at [EVENT NAME]. Write an email that:
- References a specific part of your conversation (not just "great meeting you")
- Mentions something you followed up on since the event (a question they asked, a resource they wanted)
- Connects the event topic to a specific capability you have
- Offers a call, but with a specific agenda tied to their expressed interest
- Includes a photo from the event or a link to a resource you discussed if applicable
Make it feel like a continuation of the conversation, not a cold email.
```

## Prompt 29 — Follow-Up After They Went Dark Mid-Process

```
You are a sales rep who lost momentum with [PROSPECT NAME] after [STAGE: e.g., discovery call / trial / evaluation]. Write a re-engagement email that:
- Shows you remember the specific details of their situation (use notes from your CRM)
- Identifies a possible reason for going quiet (budget cycle, internal re-org, competing priority)
- Offers exactly ONE way forward: a 10-minute call to address that specific blocker
- Includes a 1-sentence social proof from a customer who faced the same blocker
Subject line: "Tying up a loose end" or similar honest framing.
```

## Prompt 30 — Customer Success Story Follow-Up

```
You are a sales rep sending a follow-up to [PROSPECT NAME] that centers on a customer success story. Write the email:
- Subject: "[CUSTOMER] + [METRIC] in [TIMEFRAME]"
- 1-sentence intro: "Thought of you when I saw this — [CUSTOMER] was facing [SAME PROBLEM]"
- 3 bullet points showing the before/after/ROI
- 1 sentence: "Happy to connect you with [CUSTOMER CONTACT] for a 5-minute chat"
- Close: soft CTA — "Worth a quick call to see if you'd see similar results?"
```

## Prompt 31 — Follow-Up with a Specific Time Suggestion

```
You are a sales rep following up with [PROSPECT NAME] and offering specific time slots — none of which require them to check their calendar. Write an email that:
- Reminds them of the ONE value point that resonated most in your last conversation
- Offers 3 specific times (with timezone) for a 15-minute call
- Includes a 1-click calendar link as a backup
- Says "If none of these work, just reply with a better time — no need to look up your calendar"
- Is 4 sentences max. Reduce friction entirely.
```

## Prompt 32 — The "Resources" Follow-Up

```

- Asset 1: A template or framework (e.g., "ROI Calculator," "Vendor Scorecard")
- Asset 2: A video or demo snippet showing a specific use case they care about
- Asset 3: A 1-page customer story in their vertical
- One sentence per asset explaining why it's relevant to their situation
- Close: "Any of these worth a 10-minute walkthrough?" with no attachment — just links
```

```
- Lists the action items you committed to (with deadlines)
- Lists the action items THEY committed to (diplomatically)
- Attaches or links any materials discussed

Format: short paragraphs with bold headers for each section. Be the organized one.
```

```

- Opens with [REFERRER]'s name in the subject line
- States the context of the referral in one sentence
- Offers one specific way you can help based on what [REFERRER] shared
- Proposes a 10-minute call with a specific agenda
```

## Prompt 35 — The "Wrong Time" Follow-Up

```
You are a sales rep following up with [PROSPECT NAME] who previously said "not right now" or "check back in [MONTH]." It is now that month. Write a re-engagement email that:
- Shows respect for their previous timing constraint
- States what's changed on your end (new feature, pricing, customer win in their vertical)
- Asks what's changed on their end in 1 open-ended question
- Offers a 10-minute catch-up call with a specific agenda
- Under 80 words. No "I told you so" tone — pure respect and relevance.
```

## Prompt 36 — Urgency-Based Follow-Up (Limited Opportunity)

```
You are a sales rep with a legitimate time-bound reason to follow up (e.g., pricing change, implementation slot, availability of a specialist). Write an email to [PROSPECT NAME] that:
- States the deadline clearly and upfront
- Explains why it matters to them specifically (not a generic "act now")
- Recaps the expected value in 1 sentence
- Offers to handle the paperwork/admin work if they decide
- Is transparent and respectful — genuine urgency, not manufactured
Do not use fake urgency. Only use this prompt when the deadline is real.
```

## Prompt 37 — The "90-Day Nurture" Check-In

```
You are a sales rep who has been nurturing [PROSPECT NAME] for 90 days. Write a quarterly check-in email that:
- References their original goal/challenge from your first conversation
- Summarizes what's happened in the market (new trends, competitor moves, regulatory changes)
- Shares 2–3 relevant pieces of content you've published or curated
- Asks a strategic question about how their priorities have shifted
- Is positioned as a value-add update, not a sales touch
Tone: trusted advisor providing market intelligence.
```

## Prompt 38 — The "Straight Talk" Follow-Up

```
You are a sales rep who has built enough rapport with [PROSPECT NAME] for a direct approach. Write a straight-talk email that:
- Acknowledges the back-and-forth has gone on for [TIME PERIOD]
- States what you believe is holding them back (one specific reason)
- Addresses that reason directly with evidence or a solution
- Lays out the cost of inaction in 1 sentence (qualitative or quantitative)
- Asks: "Is [REASON] still a blocker, or is there something else I'm missing?"
This is for established relationships only. Use it when polite persistence has failed.
```

# 3. Objection Handling (Prompts 39–58)

## Prompt 39 — "We're Happy with Current Provider"

```
You are a sales rep handling the objection: "[PROSPECT] says they're happy with [COMPETITOR]." Write a response that:
- Acknowledges loyalty to the incumbent is rational
- Does NOT attack the competitor
- Reframes satisfaction as inertia: "What would a 20% improvement feel like?"
- Shares a 2-sentence story of a customer who felt the same way and switched after a no-risk proof of concept
- Asks: "If we could prove a [SPECIFIC METRIC] improvement in a 30-day pilot, would that be worth exploring?"
Stay classy. The goal isn't to win an argument — it's to earn a look.
```

## Prompt 40 — "It's Too Expensive" / Budget Objection

```
You are a sales rep handling a price objection from [PROSPECT NAME]. Write a response that:
- Thanks them for being upfront about budget
- Does NOT discount immediately
- Breaks down the cost into daily/per-user terms (e.g., "that's $4/day per rep")
- Reframes as ROI: "If this saves [X HOURS] per week, the payback period is [Y DAYS]"
- Offers 2 paths: (a) a scaled-down version within budget, or (b) a conversation with their manager about reallocating budget based on expected ROI
- Asks: "Which path sounds more realistic?"
Never negotiate against yourself. Let them tell you if price is the real objection.
```

## Prompt 41 — "Not a Priority Right Now"

```
You are a sales rep facing the "not a priority" objection from [PROSPECT NAME]. Write a response that:
- Validates their bandwidth constraints
- Uncovers whether it's truly a priority issue or an urgency issue
- Asks: "What would need to change for this to become a priority?"
- Quantifies the cost of delay: "Every month of delay costs approximately $[AMOUNT] in [WASTED SPEND / LOST REVENUE]"
- Proposes a 30-minute strategy session with no commitment — if it's not valuable, you'll agree it's not the right time
Tone: consultative, not pushy. Help them make a clear decision.
```

## Prompt 42 — "Send Me Information" (Information Sink)

```
You are a sales rep handling the "just send me some info" objection. Write a response that:
- Respects their request but frames information overload as a real problem
- Offers to send the 3 most relevant pieces (their choice of topics) instead of a data dump
- Asks 2 qualifying questions to narrow what's most relevant: "Would you prefer case studies or product specs? Which use case matters most?"
- Suggests a 10-minute call to review the materials together: "It'll save you 2 hours of reading"
Don't let the prospect disappear into research mode. Guide the process.
```

## Prompt 43 — "We Need to Think About It"

```
You are a sales rep handling the "we need to discuss internally" objection after a demo or proposal. Write a response that:
- Fully supports their need for internal alignment
- Offers to help build their internal business case: "Would a 1-page summary with ROI projections help your approval process?"
- Asks about the evaluation process: "Who else is involved in the decision? What would a unanimous yes look like?"
- Proposes a follow-up meeting that includes all stakeholders: "I can present the business case directly if that helps"
- Closes with: "What is the timeline for a decision so I can support accordingly?"
Help them sell internally the way they need to be sold to.
```

## Prompt 44 — "We're Too Small / Not a Fit"

```
You are a sales rep handling the "we're too small" or "we're not the right fit" objection. Write a response that:
- Acknowledges they may not fit your typical ICP
- Reframes: "Actually, some of our best results come from companies your size because [ADVANTAGE OF BEING SMALLER: e.g., faster implementation, fewer stakeholders, higher agility]"
- Shares a 2-sentence case study of a similarly sized company that saw outsized ROI
- Offers a lightweight entry point: a pilot, a module, or a flat-fee option
- Asks: "Would it be fair to explore whether fit makes sense with a 30-day pilot?"
Small companies can become big champions. Don't dismiss them.
```

## Prompt 45 — "We Built Something In-House"

```
You are a sales rep handling the "we built our own solution" objection. Write a response that:
- Compliments their engineering team's initiative
- Acknowledges the appeal of a custom build
- Gently explores the true total cost: "How many engineering hours go into maintenance each sprint?"
- Asks about roadmap gaps: "Are there features your team wants that haven't been built yet?"
- Shares the ongoing cost of bespoke vs. purpose-built: "Our customers typically find that purpose-built tools free up 2–3 engineers to work on your core product"
- Offers a side-by-side comparison: "Happy to do an apples-to-apples TCO analysis"
Never mock the homegrown solution. Engineers are proud of their work.
```

## Prompt 46 — "We Don't Have Budget Until Q[NEXT]"

```
You are a sales rep handling a "no budget until next quarter" objection. Write a response that:
- Respects their budget cycle
- Proposes a no-cost commitment now: "Let's set a calendar reminder and I'll send a monthly relevant insight so you're ready to hit the ground running"
- Offers a pilot or POC that can run in the current quarter with no purchase order required
- Asks about the planning process: "When do you start building next quarter's budget? I'd love to help with the business case."
- Secures a specific follow-up date tied to their budget planning timeline
Pipeline management means keeping warm leads warm without being pushy.
```

## Prompt 47 — "We Need to See ROI First"

```
You are a sales rep handling the "we need proof this works" objection. Write a response that:
- Agrees that ROI proof is reasonable
- Proposes a specific, measurable pilot with defined success criteria
- Offers a no-risk structure: "If we don't hit [METRIC] in [WEEKS], there's no commitment"
- Shares 3 bullet points of what the pilot would measure and how
- Includes a reference call with a customer who had the same concern
- Asks: "What metric would make this a no-brainer for you?"
Let them define success, then commit to delivering it.
```

## Prompt 48 — "We're Under Contract"

```
You are a sales rep handling the "we're locked into a contract" objection. Write a response that:
- Acknowledges the contractual constraint
- Asks about the contract end date and renewal process
- Explores dissatisfaction: "If you could change one thing about your current solution, what would it be?"
- Offers to build a transition plan: "I can design a migration timeline that aligns with your contract end"
- Asks for a future commitment: "Would you be open to a technical demo now so that when renewal comes, we're already evaluated?"
Plant the seed early. Most contract objections are timing objections in disguise.
```

## Prompt 49 — "I Need to Run This by [OTHER DECISION MAKER]"


## 🏆 Best Practices for AI Sales Prompts

- **Be specific** — the more context you give the AI, the better the output. Include industry, company size, role, and trigger events.
- **Use first-party data** — the AI can't know what your CRM says. Feed it notes from your last call, prospect behavior, and intent signals.
- **Iterate** — treat these as starting points. Tweak tone, length, and structure based on your audience.
- **Blend automation with humanity** — let the AI draft, but always add your personal touch before sending.
